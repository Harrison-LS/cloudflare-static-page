Mount Salvation. It's hard to imagine that such a name carrying religious significance corresponds to nothing more than a colorful small hill. This work of art located in the desert of Southern California has become one of the new favorite filming locations for the fashion world and entertainment industry in recent years, and is also one of the most popular travel photo spots on Instagram. If you still don't know about it, then you're really outdated! How famous is Mount Salvation actually? This artistic hill was featured in National Geographic magazine, and was listed by travel magazine "Lonely Planet" as one of 4000 must-visit destinations across the United States. Even a local California senator called it a national treasure. Although countless photos have been taken at Mount Salvation, what truly made it famous was "Paper" magazine's fashion spread shot here in March 2015, which brought this small hill in the desert to instant fame. What does Salvation Mountain really convey? About Salvation Mountain, the stories behind it are often more moving. Who is the one who built Salvation Mountain? Leonard Knight, the designer and builder of Salvation Mountain, was a veteran who experienced the Korean War. In 1984 he came to California, made a hot air balloon from old bed sheets, flew to 300 feet high where he discovered this treasure land in the desert, and then began building and coloring "Salvation Mountain". Because he carried love for God in his heart, he spent a full 24 years completing this great feat. The entire mountain is inscribed with verses from the Bible praising God, especially the prominent "God is Love," like a guiding light in the California desert. Knight building Salvation Mountain Knight painting Salvation Mountain Knights hand Knight in Creation For over 20 years, under the harsh hot and dry desert climate, he relied solely on his own hands—slowly building and painting Salvation Mountain using bricks, concrete, straw, paint, various tires, and scrap materials. In the later stages of construction, he developed an eye disease and was almost completely blind, yet he never gave up, hoping to awaken people's love for the world and their love for one another within their hearts. Eventually, Knight passed away in 2014. He left behind Salvation Mountain, which most profoundly touched people's souls. Today, a group of volunteers who share the same faith as him maintain it, opening it free of charge to visitors. Why was it built in the desert of Southern California? Salvation Mountain is located at the entrance to Slab City, like a gateway into this city. However, Slab City in America is a place that makes "normal people" avoid it at all costs. It is an "imaginary city" built by hippies among the WWII ruins in the Southern California desert. The entire area exists in a state of anarchy; "dirty, messy, and poor conditions" is probably the most fitting adjective. Lacking water, electricity, education, healthcare, and any life facilities humans need, it's even difficult to locate on maps. Yet here, retired veterans, free-spirited hippies who long for liberty, dropouts, homeless people, and even criminals proudly call it "home." Here, they freely create and express their emotions. This place is also known as "America's last truly free place." The creator Leonard Knight was also one who sought refuge here; this is also his home. The Salvation Mountain he built is like an oasis in this barren desert—a gift to God. What's Worth Seeing at Salvation Mountain Entering Salvation Mountain, it feels like stepping into a childlike world from Alice in Wonderland, with sights that catch the eye and inspire admiration everywhere. The elderly Knight painted beautiful pictures full of symbolic meaning. The paintings are filled with various elements of "love"—hearts, flowers, trees, little birds, and even rushing waterfalls, as if depicting scenes from heaven. The biggest attraction at Salvation Mountain is undoubtedly the pristine white cross atop the mountain along with the giant inscription "GOD IS LOVE." This is the essence of the entire mountain, with its striking red against the blue sky and desert backdrop, hitting directly at one's heart. Secondly recommended is a neighboring unfinished "museum" called "Branches," with colorful layered branches that resemble various cave dwellings. Their bright colors make viewers feel inner joy. Redemption Mountain is a pure handmade creation, plus the scorching desert sun causes colors to fade easily. Although volunteers are still repairing it, they cannot keep up with the rate of deterioration. So we appeal to all visitors who wish to go there to protect this artwork and not let down this old man's 24 years of effort. No high heels allowed; otherwise you'll have to walk barefoot. Follow the yellow directional signs when visiting, and avoid stepping randomly onto the mountain structure. Try not to touch with your hands as much as possible.
