Before we dive into the fun stuff, let's start with some historical background. In the 19th century, building "Route 66" brought a surge of job opportunities during America's economic depression. The completed route was smooth and easy to drive on, making it highly favored by truck drivers who used it as an important transportation corridor. Simultaneously, with Route 66's rise, regional attractions along the way began to develop—Teepee-shaped motels, Indian artifact shops, alligator farms for sightseeing visits, and more. Some businesses even started implementing creative marketing techniques. Red's Giant Hamburg's emerged in Missouri as the first fast-food restaurant and invented the "drive-through" business model, allowing travelers to purchase delicious burgers without getting out of their cars. Meanwhile, the first McDonald's was also born during this period in San Bernardino, California. This road once served as the primary route to the American West. Later, wider and flatter interstate highways replaced it—some segments were substituted while others fell into disuse entirely. So when we talk about Route 66 road trips today, we're not driving exclusively on the original Route 66. Instead, we follow its historic path, mostly traveling along modern interstates I-55, I-44, and I-40. This route contains the most concentrated Route 66 landmarks and history, allowing travelers to see various historical markers throughout their journey. You can also encounter all kinds of interesting people and things. Route 66 not only witnessed the changes in American social life in the early 20th century but has also become a historical microcosm of American modernization and the continuous exploration by the American people.
