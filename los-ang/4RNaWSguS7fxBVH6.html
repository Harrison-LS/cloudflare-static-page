Day 1: Los Angeles - Joshua Tree National Park Los Angeles to Joshua Tree National Park quickly reaches Yucca Valley through America's oldest and largest wind farm, set within a uniquely scenic landscape at Joshua Tree National Park. Joshua Tree National Park is an unusual park featuring not only giant boulders, rocky terrain, and gold mining ruins, but also desert plains dotted with strange trees. The Joshua Tree (Joshua tree) is locally nicknamed "J-Tree" and grows in an ecotone where the higher-elevation Mojave Desert meets the lower-elevation Colorado Desert. Where elevations intersect, this ecotone forms surprising desert plant communities, including this oddly-shaped tree species (which is actually a type of yucca). From magnificent sunsets to star-filled nights, Joshua Tree always radiates unique beauty. Joshua Tree National Park Joshua Tree National Park is an American national park located in Southern California. The park was established by the U.S. Congress through the 1994 "California Desert Protection Act." Prior to this, Joshua Tree had been a national monument area. Joshua Tree National Park derives its name from the Joshua tree, a large yucca that is also the representative plant of the Mojave Desert. This park protects the ecosystems of the Mojave Desert and part of the Sonoran Desert, and with its various giant rock cliffs, it is naturally heaven for climbing enthusiasts. In this small town, there is the Seligman Army Air Corps Museum, housing many World War II memorabilia; here is Seligman's oldest train history exhibition station, parked with three vintage locomotives and artifacts from America's railway boom; here is the Mojave Museum specializing in Arizona's northwestern historical sites and development. America Route 66 Route 66 stretches from the coastal city of Santa Monica all the way to the inland city of Chicago, affectionately called "Mother Road" by Americans, also known as "America's Main Street." Route 66 was built in November 1926, passing through Illinois, Missouri, Kansas, Oklahoma, Texas, New Mexico, Arizona, and California, with a total length of 3,939 kilometers. After the interstate highway system rose, Route 66's importance gradually declined, usage decreased, and it was officially removed from the U.S. National Highway System on June 27, 1985. In 1999, President Clinton signed the National Route 66 Protection Bill. Through efforts from all parties, America's Route 66 returned to the map once more. Day Three: Kingman - Grand Canyon National Park Grand Canyon National Park was included in UNESCO's World Heritage List in 1979. The grandeur of the Grand Canyon lies not only in its myriad unique peaks, strange rocks, cliffs, and rock pillars, but also in its ever-changing colors. Due to different rock properties and minerals contained on both canyon walls, they display different colors under sunlight. For example, iron ore appears colorful in various hues, other oxides show various dull tones, while quartz displays white. Grand Canyon National Park - Grand Canyon National Park is one of the world's seven natural wonders, located in northwestern Arizona. It is divided into two main sections: South Rim and North Rim, each with unique characteristics. - The South Rim is the most visited route by tourists, and its scenic drive is built quite perfectly; the North Rim has higher elevation with thinner air, but the sky is as blue as a washed cloth. - While not the deepest canyon in the world, its magnificence and breathtaking scenery rightfully claim it as number one globally, making it one of America's most worthwhile national parks to visit. While enjoying beautiful views, you can also expand your geographical knowledge. Fifth Day: Sedona-Pegee Antelope Canyon is one of the world's famous slot canyons, located in northern Arizona, USA. It was formed by millions of years of various erosive forces, primarily flash floods' erosion, followed by wind erosion. During monsoon seasons, flash floods frequently flow into the canyon. Due to sudden heavy rainfall causing quite fast flood velocities, combined with narrow channels constricting the river course, vertical erosive force also relatively increases, forming corridors at the canyon bottom and edges on canyon walls that are hard, smooth, and flowing-like. Powell Lake Powell Lake is located in Colorado and Utah states, formed by the construction of Glen Dam on the Colorado River. It is America's second largest artificial lake, named after explorer General John Wesley Powell. The lake stretches 299 kilometers long with a shoreline of 3058 kilometers. It is divided into South Powell Lake and North Powell Lake. There are many famous attractions around it, such as Glen Canyon National Recreation Area, Glen Canyon Dam, Horseshoe Bend, and Antelope Canyon in Page Town. Nowadays, resorts and campsites have been built around Powell Lake. If you have enough time, you can drive directly to the lakeside and spend several days enjoying both the lake and surrounding attractions. But if your time is limited, you can take a boat ride on the lake surface to enjoy the scenery of both shores. Some look like swords inserted straight into the clouds, while others stand elegantly like graceful young women. Stay overnight near Zion National Park. Zion National Park (Zion National Park) is a national park located in southwestern Utah, USA. This 229-square-mile park's premier attraction is Zion Canyon, stretching 15 miles long with depths reaching half a mile. Its red and tan Navajo sandstone formations are carved by tributaries of the Virgin River to the north. Other notable features include The White Dome, Checkerboard Mesa, Kolob Arch, The Three Patriarchs, and Zion Narrows. Unlike the arid beauty of Bryce Canyon National Park and Joshua Tree National Park, Zion National Park presents lush, verdant scenery with babbling brooks and birdsong echoing through the landscape. It resembles a magnificent gallery created by nature, offering endless discoveries at every step that never grow old. Located at the intersection of the Mojave Desert, Colorado Plateau, and Great Basin, this unique geographical environment supports diverse plant and animal life. According to records, 289 bird species, 75 mammal species inhabit Zion Park, while more than 800 types of plants flourish freely. The park's environment remains remarkably natural and pristine. Day Seven: Zion National Park - Valley of Fire - Las Vegas Flaming Gorge State Park is named because of the large areas of red sandstone from the Mesozoic era exposed on the surface of the valley that refract a fiery red color under sunlight, appearing like burning flames when viewed from afar. Famous for its varying shades of red sandstone formations, ancient carvings left by early humans, and unparalleled unique landscapes, it is an experience you cannot miss. Flaming Gorge State Park Flaming Gorge State Park is the first state park in Nevada, USA. Its most distinctive feature is that red sandstone can be found everywhere within its boundaries, resembling burning flames. This place was once considered sacred by local Indigenous people. On top of the red sandstone, numerous murals remain, faithfully depicting ancient Indian life and indigenous records of cosmic and stellar changes. The visitor center at the park entrance screens multimedia short films that introduce visitors to mural history and also explain the ecological environment within the park. The unique scenery has made Flaming Gorge State Park a filming location for multiple Hollywood sci-fi movies, including 2007's "Transformers" (Transformers). Coming here allows you to personally experience the passion of flames burning.
