Hotel Cecil The "Blue Fairy Case" made this Cecil Hotel located in downtown Los Angeles famous again, and also reminded people of the terrifying "Black Dahlia" unsolved case. The victim of this murder case, Elizabeth Short, was found dead on the roadside in 1947, with witnesses saying her last sighting location was the Cecil Hotel. Additionally, there are bizarre falling incidents on each floor of this hotel, and it's also a hotel where serial killers often stayed. Address: 640 S Main St, Los Angeles, CA 90014 Cecil Hotel Knickerbocker Hotel The Knickerbocker Hotel was originally built in 1925 as an apartment building before being converted into a hotel. Rumors claim that Rudolph Valentino's ghost frequently haunts the bar of this hotel, while Marilyn Monroe is often seen wandering the women's restroom. Another maintenance worker told reporters that the ghost of a woman who once jumped from this building also appears regularly in the hotel, "Many people have seen her. While I was working in the basement, shadows would drift by and doors would close automatically." But the most famous ghost never actually appeared here. After Harry Houdini died on Halloween in 1926, his widow Beth held séances annually on the roof of the Knickerbocker Hotel for a decade, trying to contact him, but he never made an appearance. Address: 1714 Ivar Ave, Los Angeles, CA 90028 Hollywood Sign A jogger once reported seeing what appeared to be a blonde woman walking through the air near the Hollywood Sign. This was actress Peg Entwistle—or more accurately, her ghost. Career failures left Peg Entwistle deeply depressed, and on one day in September 1932, she chose to jump from the "H" of the Hollywood Sign, ending her life. After that incident, it became common for people to report seeing women dressed in period clothing with attire reminiscent of Peg Entwistle wandering near the Hollywood Sign. **Hollywood Sign** • When one thinks of Hollywood, the image that naturally comes to mind is the massive HOLLYWOOD letters standing atop a hill. • Originally, the full name read HOLLYWOOD LAND and served as an advertising slogan for the Hollywood film industry. • After 1960, the four letters LAND were removed, leaving the familiar HOLLYWOOD sign we recognize today. • The only way to take commemorative photos with it is from Griffith Observatory nearby or certain areas within the city. **Queen Mary** This cruise ship hotel in Long Beach was rated by Time magazine as one of America's top 10 most haunted locations. The hotel officially offers ghost tours that can be booked through their website. It is said that the ghost of an engineer who died inside the engine room still roams the vessel. In the unused first-class swimming pool area, women wearing 1930s swimsuits are seen wandering on deck, and people have also spotted a young girl carrying a teddy bear. In the second-class swimming pool, visitors report sightings of both a little girl who drowned and a woman in an evening gown. In several rooms in first class, people have even seen a man from the 1830s appear, accompanied by flickering lights, sounds of running water, and phone ringing. The baby's room on third class often emits the cries of infants. Address: 1126 Queens Hwy, Long Beach, CA 90802 Hollywood Roosevelt Hotel Roosevelt competed with Knierbocker for most opportunities where celebrities would appear. Marilyn Monroe was reported to have appeared in a full-length mirror in "Suite 1200", where she stayed when she was just starting her career. There are also claims that Montgomery Clift wandered around room 928 and the surrounding area, where he stayed while filming "Molloy". A medium reportedly found Humphrey Bogart, Carmen Miranda, and Betty Grable as well. Near the "Blossom Room", it is said a well-dressed man from the 1930s can be seen wandering around. Address: 7000 Hollywood Blvd, Los Angeles, CA 90028 The Alexandria Hotel 1926's Alexander is now a low-income apartment complex, yet even in downtown's most upscale area, it remains an old, dilapidated building. It is said that several dancer ghosts wander through the second-floor dance hall, while an angry teenager roams Charlie Chaplin's former suite. Rudolph Valentino occasionally left Knickerbocker Hotel to visit his 12-story apartment. Tunnels crisscross the basement, and reportedly a few bandit ghosts also reside there. Address: 501 S Spring St, Los Angeles, CA 90013 Colorado Street Bridge This 1912 bridge spanning the Arroyo Seco River was once part of Route 66, but since the 1930s it has been infamous as "Suicide Bridge." In 1919, someone first jumped from the bridge. Since then, at least one hundred people have followed suit, many during the Great Depression (and still about 10 annually). It is believed that several ghosts inhabit this bridge, possibly including a construction worker who fell into wet concrete. Address: 504 W Colorado Blvd, Pasadena, CA 91105 Colorado Street Bridge The Colorado Street Bridge is one of the places where Mia and Sebastian had their romantic summer dates. In reality, this bridge also serves as a transportation hub connecting downtown Los Angeles with Old Pasadena. In the film, it became a place where the two protagonists would stroll and share their feelings with each other. The Colorado Bridge is 1,500 feet long. This curved-design bridge serves as a transportation hub connecting Los Angeles and Old Pasadena, and is the final stop of the famous Route 66. Every July, grand party events are held here. Los Angeles City Hall The Los Angeles City Hall building is the center for all activities of local government employees in this state. This building was constructed in 1928 and houses 32 floors of offices and conference rooms, as well as the mayor's office. It is said that there were ghost stories long ago. According to reports, it once housed a morgue. Security personnel particularly claim that the 2nd, 3rd, 4th, 27th, and 28th floors are the most haunted, saying you can truly feel someone walking beside you. The camera even captured unexplained figures in the ghostly hall. Address: 200 N Spring St, Los Angeles, CA 90012 Los Angeles City Hall This is where the Los Angeles municipal government carries out its work. The city hall, built in 1928, was the tallest building on the West Coast at that time and held this distinction for over two decades. All the main construction materials were sourced from local California soil and water sources. The tower design atop the building emulates the Mausoleum of Maussollos (one of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World), giving it unique characteristics. This has made it a popular filming location for many Hollywood movies, including the well-known Superman film. Unlike government buildings elsewhere that are heavily guarded and difficult to approach, this one is more accessible. The spherical hall on the first floor, along with some office areas and meeting rooms, are free to visit by the public. Visitors can also tour the state government press conference venues, all under the guidance of staff members. If you're interested in American politics, want to understand how a state government operates, or simply wish to see inside a government building's layout, the Los Angeles City Hall is definitely worth visiting. Mission San Buenaventura It is said that every church in California harbors ghosts, but only one has ghost cats! Father Francisco Uria kept four pet cats he loved very much. After his death, the four cats solemnly made their way toward the chapel. They jump onto the church bell ropes, swinging back and forth, delivering mournful news of the pastor's death. Sometimes you can even hear their cries and footsteps. There are also reports of monks' ghosts appearing in the church and courtyard. It is said that these monk spirits only manifest before fellow believers. Address: 211 E Main St, Ventura, CA 93001 Bullocks Wilshire Building As early as 1929, this was once a luxury department store where celebrities like Alfred Hitchcock and John Wayne frequently shopped. Now the building belongs to Western University's Law School. But before it was purchased, this structure had a dark and unforgettable past. Rumors say that as early as the 1930s, a woman died after a man pushed her from an elevator shaft ledge. During renovations, security personnel reported hearing a girl crying, seemingly emerging from inside the elevator shaft. Visitors have also witnessed lights flickering on and off by themselves, curtains closing autonomously, and heard footsteps echoing through the lobby. In the 1980s, this building appeared in the film "Poltergeist." Address: 3050 Wilshire Blvd, Los Angeles, CA 90005 That concludes LA's top ten genuinely haunted locations.
