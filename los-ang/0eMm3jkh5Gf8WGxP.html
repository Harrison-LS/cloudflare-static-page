Salvation Mountain is located at the entrance to Slab City, a place once called "post-apocalyptic" by people. Some also say it is America's last truly free place. Why is that? Because in the 1980s, during an economic crisis, retired veterans abandoned by society, hippies, hobos, and others chose to come here. And its creator, Leonard Knight, was also part of this exodus. He was a veteran who had experienced the Korean War, coming here with "redemption" and "faith." Using old bed sheets, he crafted a hot air balloon, flew it 300 feet into the sky, and immediately discovered this tilted hill—three stories high, 100 feet long, made of concrete and artificial adobe bricks. Knight then began building on and painting this mountain. He used another form to express his love for God, hoping to awaken people's inner love for the world and for each other. Enduring the desert's scorching heat and dry climate, he lived with a group of homeless cats in his dilapidated truck. Every day, he painted on the mountain. His surreal painting style combined with the torn, desolate desert created an atmosphere reminiscent of apocalyptic carnival. For the next 24 years, Knight lived here, relying solely on his own strength to gradually build and paint Salvation Mountain using bricks, concrete, straw, paint, various tires, and scrap materials. Throughout this period, he faced environmentalists' protests, government approvals, fans' support, and the challenge of his eye disease. Yet none of these could hinder his persistence and passion in building "Salvation Mountain." He believed it was both an offering to God from himself and a gift for every visitor. Even as he approached total blindness, he insisted on personally improving Salvation Mountain. Eventually, Knight passed away in 2014, leaving us with the heart-touching Salvation Mountain and those words: "God Is Love" — meaning God is love. Many people found shelter and peace of their souls at "Salvation Mountain."
