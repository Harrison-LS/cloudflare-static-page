Los Angeles

Brief

Route 66, affectionately known as the "Mother Road" by Americans, was a diagonal highway stretching from Chicago all the way to Santa Monica in California. Opened for use on November 11, 1926, it enjoyed half a century of prosperity before being officially removed from the U.S. highway system on June 27, 1985. Michael Wallis, a scholar who has studied Route 66 for more than sixty years, said: "Route 66 serves as a clear mirror to the American people; it symbolizes the arduous journey that great Americans have endured."

Content

Here we are on Route 66. You might think it is simply a road, but in reality, it is literature, song, flowing American spirit. Kerouac was mad, and now we are on our way. Humming "Blowing in the Wind," making stops over 2500 miles, stories on wheels begin. If one day you will eventually tread this road of freedom, remember to stay at those story-filled motels, theme hotels, and bed & breakfasts along the journey; stories are everywhere on the road. Key Word | Motel A motel originates from the English word "Motel," an abbreviation for motor hotel. The biggest difference between a motel and a regular hotel lies in that motels provide parking spaces directly connected to rooms. Each room has its own exclusive parking spot at the entrance. This individual design offers more privacy and is also convenient for self-driving travelers. In contrast, most motels on mainland China are actually chain business hotels. Motels in Japan and Taiwan regions often serve as synonymous with love hotels. Next time you choose a hotel for your trip, remember how to distinguish them. 1. A Motel at the Birthplace of Route 66 Speaking of which, Springfield, Missouri has an indelible historical relationship with Route 66. In April 1926, U.S. officials first proposed naming a new road from Chicago to Los Angeles as "Route 66," and this event took place right here. Therefore, Springfield is known as the birthplace of Route 66. The motel beside the historic Route 66 is named Route 66 Harbor. During the heyday of this highway, Elvis Presley once stayed here. Although it has since been acquired by the Best Western hotel chain, its interior design still preserves the original style from back then. Designed with a 1950s theme, the hotel maintains the gas station that Elvis once used here, along with several restored vintage cars. The room decorations are simple, but nostalgia is also a style, right? Campervan Family Heritage Holbrook is a railway town dating back to 1881. In 1912, this small town experienced a meteor shower. Ever since then, shops selling meteorite fragments can be seen all along the roadside. Passing Hopi Road, you can see the famous tent motel. Underneath the white sign with black letters reads: "Have you slept in a tent recently?" A few Indian-style cement buildings shaped like tents, several old cars, gravel scattered on the ground. This motel began in 1950 and has been operated by the same family to this day. Historically, there were seven tent motels built with this design. Ranked by construction date, this one is number six. Now only three remain throughout the United States—two along Route 66, another in California. The rooms are small but extremely popular. Being unable to book even half a month in advance, coming all the way by car seems reasonable enough. Feminist Pioneers of the Queen Traveling along Route 66 and crossing an iron bridge, we arrived in St. Louis—a major city in eastern Missouri—and chose The Cheshire Hotel as our place to rest. Hearing "Cheshire" immediately brings to mind the Cheshire Cat from Alice's Adventures in Wonderland, who can disappear at will. Indeed, this is a traditional hotel with strong British charm. Throughout the ground-floor lobby, one can see art portraits of the British royal family everywhere. The room keys also feature thick wooden pendants. Upon opening each door, every guest room specially provides information about a British writer and their works. A good themed hotel should be exactly like this—beyond artistic decoration, it should bring rich cultural depth. Opening the paper book on the chaise lounge in the guest room reveals Virginia Woolf, a pioneer of 20th-century modernism and feminism. Her masterpieces include "Mrs. Dalloway" and "To the Lighthouse." If staying at the hotel for a few days, why not read one during pleasant afternoons? Looking closely at the "Do Not Disturb" sign on the door might reveal surprises. Ranch Hotel's Western Cowboy Movies Gallup, a small town in New Mexico state, was the filming location for many famous Western films throughout history, including "Billy the Kid" (1930), "The Chase" (1947), "Springtime on the Loess Plateau" (1947), "Brave Man" (1951), "King of Poker" (1951), and "Thundering Horns" (1964). In 1937, director D.W. Griffith, who once directed "The Birth of a Nation," built this hotel together with his brother. Afterward, it became a luxurious residence for Western film stars in the wilderness. Entering the hotel reveals an old yet elegant lobby with wooden beams and pillars, hanging lamps, a double spiral staircase, stone fireplace, Native American carpets, deer heads, and bull horns - everywhere radiates a Western atmosphere. Walking up to the second floor along the red carpet on the stairs, black-and-white photographs of many Hollywood film stars hang on the walls. However, the close relationship between Rancho Hotel and Hollywood has become history with the decline of Western films. The last filming took place in 1994 when part of the scenes for "Natural Born Killers" was shot in this small town. Light streams through the windows - imagine a day long ago, Humphrey leaning by the window lighting a cigarette, while Croft gazing at a desert rainstorm from the same angle on another day. In the early 1960s, farms in sparsely populated areas of southwestern and central England adopted bed-and-breakfast family-style hospitality to increase income. However, whether homestays are legal has long been a continuing debate, both in China and America. 5. Spanish Immigrants Within Adobe Buildings Route 66 passes through various colorful towns. Nevertheless, even reaching the interesting New Mexico state, one cannot help but deviate from the main route to explore freely nearby. Driving towards Taos town in the mountains, this place features an Indian ancient village with a 1000-year history. Turning onto Kit Carson Street, a beautiful Adobe building appeared before our eyes, marked as Benavides' House. The hostess is a descendant of Spanish immigrants, holding a small baby in her arms, rocking it gently while handling our check-in procedures for us. The combination of earth-yellow walls and sky-blue doors creates an excellent sense of harmony. The room decorations are also filled with rough Indian atmosphere. However, due to limited internet access, most of my time staying here was spent lounging on the living room sofa to get Wi-Fi while also enjoying the bright sunlight streaming through the floor-to-ceiling glass windows. In Taos Town, there are countless hotels of all sizes. If you have ample time, I'd recommend finding a homestay in an adobe structure building, chatting with the hosts about this small town—experiencing both traditional Native American architecture and the comfort of modern life. I stayed at Casa Benavides for three days straight, enjoying free breakfasts and afternoon tea treats while listening to the owners share their story of immigrating from Spain. Only when you slow your travel pace down to a lifestyle can you truly savor these experiences. A 20th Century Train Station Inn If asked which area along Route 66 offers the most fascinating homestays, New Mexico stands out as an undeniable top choice. In Albuquerque, the state's largest city, I ended up staying in this charming historic train station lodging. The owners of the homestay, Jerry and his wife, share a common hobby—renovating old houses. In this historic city, they have renovated many old buildings, so this 1902-built train station inn was transformed into its current form: two floors with six rooms in total, and even Jerry's wedding dress is displayed at the entrance. You can cook your own meals and even host parties. The only drawback is no shower. On check-in day, we were the only guests. Jerry left me the keys and went off. All rooms are nice— which one should I choose? Tent Homestay Where You Sleep With Mythical Creatures In Santa Fe, New Mexico, escape the tourist-filled downtown and go to sleep alongside llamas in the nearby desert. This is Farmer Bill's place. He spent a year building this tent. Bathing relies on a water tank that only lasts about thirteen minutes. Even waste disposal requires "manual sifting." But Bill says this is my resistance against resource wastage in urbanization. After all, living in the desert means we have an obligation to conserve every possible drop of water. Staying in Bills tent offers one of the most unique experiences imaginable. There are no beds inside the tent. Bill performs like a magician by assembling a space-cotton chair into a mattress. Then he pulls out a quilt from a bamboo box nearby, and a simple bed is complete. If you find it stuffy inside the tent, why not come sit on the balcony to see a herd of llamas nearby? Oh, and if you happen to be downwind, you might even catch a whiff of their scent. In the morning, you can spend $15 on a llama walking experience. Walking down the street with Bill leading llamas is simply too cool for words. For these curious creatures, being taken out for a walk is extremely enjoyable. Today accompanying us was a two-year-old named "Obama." After affectionately sniffing each other, they began their pleasant stroll. Oh by the way, remember to wear an extra protective layer; otherwise this two-hour journey through the desert is enough to bake another T-shirt onto your skin. Living in a Hollywood Director's Home Film enthusiasts visiting California first think of the Universal Studios Hollywood theme park. Although "Hollywood" has become synonymous with the film industry, it remains an area name in the Los Angeles suburbs. Finding and staying at a guesthouse that raises three cats near the beach at the end of Route 66 in Santa Monica, I discovered I was actually living in a director's home. That day happened to be the Dragon Boat Festival. I gave them white zongzi that I had just bought from Chinatown, saying, "This is food from the dragon." Lisa and Joe were about to drive out for their son's birthday party, exclaiming, "Great! We'll give this food as a gift to him." A white cat lay quietly on the soft chair in the kitchen, watching us come and go, occasionally flicking its tail. Our room had been renovated by the inn owners. To get from the living room to the bedroom, you had to pass a whole wall filled with books about film production. The owner Joe grew up in Hollywood; he directed a World War II movie that can be seen on Youku. After learning we were from China, Joe remarked that the Yuan Dynasty truly killed too many people.
