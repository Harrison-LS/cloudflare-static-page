Los Angeles is one of Americas most diverse cities, with immigrants from around the world crossing space and time to arrive and take root; while adapting to the lifestyle and pace of life on this new continent, they have also preserved the customs and traditions of their homelands; and Los Angeles respect and inclusivity toward multiple ethnic groups has created the rich social and cultural landscape this city enjoys today. Counting them up, there are no fewer than twenty-plus cultural districts in Los Angeles - truly you can tour the world without even leaving the state, all within Los Angeles. Today we will start our "Asian Tour"~ Little Tokyo Little Tokyo There are only three officially recognized Japanese-American cultural districts nationwide: San Francisco, San Jose, and Little Tokyo in Los Angeles. In LA, Little Tokyo holds significant importance; even out-of-town tourists specifically request a visit to Little Tokyo, making it virtually an extension of Japanese spiritual culture on the ground. Currently, Little Tokyo primarily serves as a commercial and cultural activity center. Dining Little Tokyos ramen shops beyond the popular Daikokuya spicy miso ramen include Men Ohs Tokushima Ramen which also receives rave reviews! Those who appreciate the chewy texture of udon noodles simply cannot miss authentic Marugame Monzo, this famous queue-worthy specialty shop? If you're craving refreshing sushi, Sushi Gen is so popular everyone knows about it, with crowds every day. If you don't want to queue, try Oomasa or Sushi Go for their excellent sashimi rice bowls, which are equally amazing. Japanese desserts always evoke that girly heart feeling - go to Cafe Dulce for matcha doughnuts with coffee, or visit Beard Papa's for cream puffs. Little Tokyo Little Tokyo is named so because every aspect - from street scenes, restaurant shops, window displays, entertainment venues, to the people walking through them - is filled with Japanese flavor. It's also a uniquely exotic area of Los Angeles. Little Tokyo can be easily explored on foot. Its history dates back about 100 years, becoming prosperous after World War I. However, it was damaged during World War II. In recent years, after being restored and reorganized, it has become its current lively and cohesive appearance, showcasing both 19th-century commercial architecture and modern housing design. Little Tokyos annual major event is Nisei Week, held every August featuring performances such as flower arranging, tea ceremony, calligraphy, Japanese art, parades, street entertainment, and more. Besides shopping for Japanese goods, Little Tokyo is the best place in Los Angeles to try Japanese cuisine, with many sushi restaurants, izakayas, and various forms of entertainment at night including karaoke. Little Osaka Sawtelle It seems the rivalry between eastern and western Japan has spread to Los Angeles as well. Near UCLA, Sawtelle has gathered a significant number of people of Japanese descent, gradually forming a concentrated Japanese community. Because it is located on the more western side, it eventually earned the title "Little Osaka." There are many Japanese shops and restaurants here. Many businesses open until midnight. Most parking lots offer convenient and free parking. It's the "midnight diner" most favored by students. Dining For example, the famous ramen shop Menya Musashi also opened in July 2018. The ramen features thick slices of pork belly! Japanese-style BBQ Manpuku Tokyo BBQ's beef tongue is incredibly satisfying~ Kitchen Story is a favorite among many international students. Not only that, but having a Taiwanese shaken drink at Coco Fresh Tea & Juice after eating and drinking enough would make it perfect. Now, the area is actually "defying trends," embracing a more artistic, bohemian style. Walk through to experience Chinese-American history and culture—see traditional archways, red lanterns hanging high above, bronze statues of Sun Yat-sen and Bruce Lee, and signage mixing Chinese and English characters. Address: Simply type "Chinatown Los Angeles, CA" Los Angeles Chinatown ·Los Angeles' Chinatown connects to the old town district and is one of several major Chinese communities along America's West Coast. ·Built in the Chinese architectural style from the 1970s, it features an elegant traditional Chinese archway at the main entrance, with red lanterns lining the streets and shop signs written entirely in Chinese characters. ·Visitors can enjoy authentic Chinese cuisine here, purchase unique Chinese gifts, and watch special dragon and lion dance performances during holidays. ·In this area filled with Chinese people, you can completely escape English constraints and experience warm hometown feelings far from home. ·Today's Chinatown no longer belongs exclusively to Chinese people—immigrants from various Asian countries love living here, making the district gradually become a symbol of American recognition and respect for Asian culture. Little Taipei - Monterey Park In the 1980s, a large number of Taiwanese immigrants came to Monterey Park, giving it the nickname "Little Taipei." Today, Monterey Park is actually the largest Chinese American settlement in greater Los Angeles. Dining Taiwanese-style breakfast shop Huge Tree Pastry's soy milk buns and pork floss rice balls are a major specialty here! If you want to eat xiaolongbao (soup dumplings) but don't want to wait in line at Din Tai Fung, then come try Mama's Lu Dumpling House. As for friends who love tea, you should all know Elite Restaurant. The prices are slightly higher but portions are larger and the dishes are more exquisite. As for those who like barbecue, you definitely cannot miss Dasheng BBQ where a long line of people queues up daily. Los Angeles's best barbecue - give it a try! It originated from Westminster, which has the nickname "Little Saigon," and gradually expanded into the Garden Grove area. More than thirty percent of residents in these two cities are of Vietnamese descent, making it the most concentrated Vietnamese population region in America. Dining In addition to Vietnamese cuisine, Garden Grove and Westminster have many popular dessert shops/hipster-style coffee shops in recent years. For example, The Loop: Handcrafted Churros churro ice cream, Mr Matcha with delicious matcha flavors, and colorful Sweet Rolled Tacos shaved ice tacos are all worth trying. Just north of Little Armenia lies Los Angeles' Thai Town, covering about six city blocks - the only officially named Thai community in America. Stepping into this neighborhood reveals lavish and colorful Thai-style decorations. At the western entrance stands a statue of Apsonsi, a guardian deity with a human face and animal body. Walking along Hollywood Boulevard through Thai Town, you'll see rows of beautiful yellow-flowering trees. For a Thai massage bath experience, relax at Thai Sabai. Dining The Thai Town Square area offers many choices for Thai cuisine. Ruen Pair is authentic but accepts cash only If you want specialty Thai barbecue, go to The Original Thai BBQ Finally, don't forget to order Bhan Kanom Thai's mango sticky rice and coconut pudding as dessert after the meal. And before heading home, don't forget to buy some of their Thailand-exclusive flavored potato chips! It features numerous Filipino church buildings, including the Filipino Christian Church which has been listed as a Los Angeles Historic-Cultural Monument. Dining When visiting Little Philippines, you cannot miss trying halo-halo, a traditional Filipino shaved ice dessert with colorful layers of ingredients that provides refreshing relief in hot weather, available at authentic Filipino restaurant LA Rose Cafe. Of course, visitors should also order the classic Filipino breakfast combination of sunny-side-up egg and grilled pork rice. The Park's Finest presents Filipino cuisine with an American style, frequently rated by magazines as one of Los Angeles' best BBQ restaurants. Filipino-style barbecue is savory-sweet and highly appetizing, featuring signature corn cakes for you to enjoy.
