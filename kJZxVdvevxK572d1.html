Top 10. Hollywood Forever Cemetery Eternal Hollywood, a gathering place for hippies. Hollywood Forever Cemetery is LA's oldest cemetery, situated at the heart of the city. Established in 1899, it can be considered one of Los Angeles' most ancient cemeteries, covering 62 acres. The cemetery rests more than 90,000 souls, including 62 top-tier Hollywood celebrities. Neatly manicured lawns and solemn monuments all bear witness to the final moments of countless Hollywood favorites. Today, the cemetery plays an even greater role in hosting large-scale events, including film screenings and concerts. For example, Breaking Bad held its farewell event here. During summer months, there are weekly outdoor movie screenings, and occasionally small nighttime performances by independent bands. Girls wearing Bohemian-style fairy dresses come and go, making it perfect for people-watching. As Coco puts it, as long as we have not forgotten, their stories and spirits will live on forever. Address: 6000 Santa Monica Blvd, Los Angeles, CA 90038 WE WELCOME EVERYONE. Completed in 1926, the Los Angeles Central Library is Los Angeles' oldest building and currently the third largest library in America. Even if you have no interest in reading at all, this library is worth a visit. The building features Egyptian and Mediterranean architectural styles, with a vibrant pyramid-shaped roof that reflects the city's contemporary passion for Egyptian architecture at the time. The interior decoration on the second floor is even more breathtaking. The dome blends Art Deco with Arab motifs, along with large murals depicting California's history and an exquisite crystal chandelier. Library opens at 10 AM. Many people wait in the small garden to enter as soon as it opens, whether they are scholars or homeless individuals. The inscription "reading is beneficial" on the fence and the "we welcome everyone" signs posted throughout the interior make this building shine with humanistic brilliance. Address: 630 W 5th St, Los Angeles, CA 90071 Free guided tours: Mon-Fri 12:30; set 11:00-14:00, Sun 14:00 Los Angeles Central Library Completed in 1926, the Los Angeles Central Library is not only Los Angeles' oldest building but also a masterpiece of classical architecture. Designed by renowned architect Bertram Grosvenor Goodhue, it combines architectural design with sculpture and painting art. It represented a bold breakthrough among American public buildings at the time, especially the torch atop the pyramid-shaped roof which symbolizes the fire of seeking knowledge, resonating with the spirit of the Central Library. Top8. Marciano Art Foundation Church or Gallery This Marciano Art Museum on Wilshire Boulevard was originally a Freemason church. The building still retains Masonic symbols and statues, with even one exhibition hall dedicated to displaying Masonic artifacts. However, after renovation, this place mainly collects modern art with bold and passionate styles. There are familiar artists like Yayoi Kusama, as well as avant-garde artists whose names neither you nor I can recall. History, religion, and contemporary art merge in a unique way here. Address: 4357 Wilshire Blvd, Los Angeles, CA 90010 Tickets: Free but requires advance online reservation Top7. Annenberg Space for Photography Photos record the most authentic stories. This writer first learned about this place through its World Refugee Exhibition, and it has since become one of LA's regular spots to visit. Annenberg Photography Space is Los Angeles area's first pure photography cultural venue, with interior design inspired by the mechanical structure of cameras and lenses. The central circular digital gallery is housed within the plaza building, featuring a striking iris-like design on the ceiling reminiscent of camera apertures. Colorful photography exhibitions are held annually, making it an excellent place for both viewing exhibits and taking photos. Even though it's located in Century City's business district, its innovative and avant-garde displays and installations attract many art lovers. Griffith Observatory Overlooking the entire city of Los Angeles, Hollywood's best filming location Those who've watched "La La Land" will find themselves enamored by Griffith Observatory. This world-famous observatory with over eighty years of history sits atop a mountain in northwest Los Angeles. The dome in the main hall features charming murals, along with installations such as the Foucault pendulum, Tesla coil, and planetarium. After dark, the stargazing area opens free to visitors, offering views of countless stars above. After reaching the observatory, continue hiking up toward the summit for the most breathtaking views of Los Angeles, especially during those glorious nighttime illuminations. Central City, Glendale, and West Hollywood - the entire Los Angeles city visible at once. PS: Evening temperatures on the mountain are very low; remember to bring a jacket. Address: 2800 E Observatory Rd, Los Angeles, CA 90027 Griffith Observatory · Located on the northwest hilltop of Los Angeles, offering panoramic views of the entire city. · Exhibits astronomical knowledge about planets and the universe; features a dome theater at the top modeled after the sky where visitors can enjoy 360° films depicting celestial evolution. Bare-eye 3D technology brings cosmic wonders within reach. · The open-air platform overlooks Los Angeles, with orderly streets, neat urban architecture, and downtown skyscrapers all clearly visible. · After sunset, the observatory opens free of charge, allowing views of twinkling stars above and lunar surface features. Top 5. Venice Canals Take photos and stroll to experience Venetian canal atmosphere Though named after canals, this area is more like an exotic cultural district. Over 100 years ago, developer Abbot Kinney planned to build an "American Venice" in California; however, with the rise of the Industrial Revolution, the area gradually declined. Enter from a small sign reading "Venice Canals Sidewalk" at the corner of Washington Boulevard and Strong Drive. Buildings of various styles from different periods line along the riverbank. Some gardens hang strings of star-like neon lights; some doorsteps display exquisite sets of chairs and tables; tall palm trees stand proudly; colorful flowers bloom in abundance. Gardens and small bridges are scattered throughout, with pointed boats occasionally docked by the river. Everywhere, scents of lavender and rosemary drift through the air. Passing by are not just houses, but different facets of life. Address: Venice, CA 90291 Top 4: Getty Villa "One Getty, Two Locations." J. Paul Getty, a self-made millionaire who wrote a book called "How to be Rich," truly embodied the rags-to-riches story. Yet this nouveau riche had an innocent heart that loved art. The Getty Villa in Malibu became a museum when he opened his private collection of over 4000 artifacts from ancient Egypt, Greece, and Rome at his villa to the public. Unlike the modern feel of the Getty Center, the water-side villa follows a classical route. These exhibits are divided by themes including gods, goddesses, and the Trojan War, extending to oil paintings from the era of Louis XVI, spanning dates from 6500 BC to AD 500. Yes, the Getty Villa takes the Greek classic approach, serving as a refreshing alternative in Los Angeles where modern art is abundant. Address: 17985 Pacific Coast Hwy, Pacific Palisades, CA 90272 Getty Villa Top. #3 Getty Center "If I could, I would like to sit in the center garden and just stare blankly for an afternoon." Compared with Getty Villa's grand and elegant architectural style, Getty Center appears more imposing and modern. The all-white building complex was constructed on a cliff 881 feet high. Clean lines, bright colors, natural lighting, indoor courtyards seamlessly integrated with outdoor gardens; open spaces that combine delicate and rough aesthetic beauty harmoniously. Six exhibition halls are scattered among the visible greenery—each corner offers different scenery, you can wander around and stop to enjoy for a whole day. The way up is by taking the tram, along the route you can see views of all of West LA. Getty Center's exhibits are also more modern than its sister museum Getty Villa. With 50,000 collections dating from the Middle Ages onward, it includes many Renaissance period masterpieces, including famous works by Rubens, Cézanne and Van Gogh, among others. Top.2 Hammer Museum "Visit exhibitions & capture memorable moments." Hammer Museum is also a private art gallery established by American businessman Armand Hammer in 1990, mainly to house his personal collection. Like J. Paul Getty, it reflects wealth with passion for collecting. The museum primarily collects Impressionist and Post-Impressionist works. Its holdings include 7,500 pieces from French satirical artist Honoré Daumier, along with 17th-century European masterpieces by Rembrandt, late Italian Renaissance works by Titian, and 18th-century French still life masterpieces by Chardin. Today at the Hammer Museum focuses on exhibiting works by local Los Angeles artists across various forms including paintings, photographs, films, and art installations, offering unique artistic experiences. The museum also frequently hosts film screenings—some visitors have even encountered Quentin there. Address: 10899 Wilshire Blvd, Los Angeles, CA 90024 Opening Hours: Monday | CLOSED Tuesday - Friday | 11 a.m.–8 p.m. Saturday & Sunday | 11 a.m.–5 p.m. Hammer Museum #1 The Broad Museum "Infinity Mirror Rooms" This modern art museum located on Grand Avenue in downtown has been open for three years and remains popular to this day. Its continued appeal is largely due to Yayoi Kusama's Infinity Mirror Rooms—ethereal and dreamlike. Although there's always a queue, it's undeniable that it's an excellent spot for taking photos. From exterior to interior, various wormhole-style wall designs create intrigue throughout the museum on the third floor. The ceiling reaches 23 feet high, featuring 318 skylights that allow natural light to filter and scatter through these roof openings. From outside to inside, the entire building embodies a surreal futuristic aesthetic. In addition, this space houses more than two thousand pieces from the Broad Collection, including works by modernist pioneers such as Andy Warhol, Barbara Kruger, and Jean-Michel Basquiat. While Basquiat's name may sound unfamiliar to some, his paintings sold for an impressive $110 million at auction last year. Adjacent is the Walt Disney Concert Hall, which you can also visit. Address: 221 S Grand Ave, Los Angeles, CA 90012 Opening Hours: Monday | CLOSED Tuesday & Wednesday | 11 a.m.–5 p.m. Thursday & Friday | 11 a.m.–8 p.m. Saturday | 10 a.m.–8 p.m. Sunday | 10 a.m.–6 p.m. Walt Disney Concert Hall Walt Disney Concert Hall is like a pulsating note of this city. This concert hall, built by renowned architectural master Frank Gehry, appears as if numerous twisting and winding steel plates have been deliberately stacked and combined with great creativity. It not only incorporates the strength of steel but also exudes elegance and softness, successfully elevating the building into a work of art and becoming a landmark in downtown Los Angeles. This concert hall serves as the home base for the Los Angeles Philharmonic, one of the world's top orchestras. In March 2015, they just completed their three-month Asian tour that included performances in Shanghai and Hong Kong, China. The massive performance hall can accommodate nearly 3,000 audience members and performers simultaneously. World-class symphony concerts are held here regularly. Visitors can purchase concert tickets or admission passes through the official website or ticket office at the concert hall. Guided tours are also available to lead visitors on a detailed exploration of this highly modernistic masterpiece's interior.
